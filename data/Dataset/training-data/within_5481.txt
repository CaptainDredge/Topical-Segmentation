WASHINGTON  (Reuters) - The G7 communiqu released in Washington repeated a call for exchange rates to reflect economic fundamentals and cited China again by name, using the exact same wording as a statement issued after a meeting in Essen, Germany, in February.In post-G7 news conferences, French Finance Minister Thierry Breton went further than others when commenting on the basic G7 message, but the collective G7 line remained that markets should take account of more balanced global growth, with U.S. activity easing, Europe going strong and Japan's recovery continuing.
