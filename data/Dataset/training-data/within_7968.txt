Stocks bounced back into positive territory as oil price rises boosted demand for energy companies and as investors bought beaten-down shares after a three-day slide. The Dow Jones industrial average ended the day up more than 90 points, or 0.68 percent, at 13,427.73. Treasury prices turned lower on the late rally in stocks.

Wall Street economists had expected orders to fall just 1 percent and the data initially boosted prices for U.S. government bonds and weighed on stocks and the dollar.

It was the first drop since January and reflected weak demand for a wide array of products, including goods businesses need to expand. Separate reports showed Midwest factory activity slipped slightly in May, while U.S. mortgage applications fell last week for the second straight week as interest rates held near recent highs and made home buying more expensive.

-----

WASHINGTON  (Reuters) - New orders for long-lasting U.S.-made manufactured goods tumbled sharply in May, the government said on Wednesday, raising doubts about the strength of the factory sector and business expansion plans.Orders for durable goods, items meant to last at least three years, slid a larger-than-expected 2.8 percent after a 1.1 percent April rise, a Commerce Department report showed.

-----

Reports earlier this week showed sales of existing U.S. homes slipped 0.3 percent to their lowest level in four years in May, while new home sales dropped 1.6 percent. Federal Reserve officials were certain to discuss both the stress in the housing market and the direction of business spending at a two-day meeting on Wednesday and Thursday that was widely expected to end with a decision to hold interest rates steady.
